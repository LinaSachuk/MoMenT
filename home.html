The proposed project is dealing with the manufacturing branch of EU economy. The particular focus within the manufacturing is placed on the metal cutting, machining and tooling. In Sweden alone the value created by these areas is immense with € 60 billion turnover for manufacturing industries directly generated through machining plus another € 12.5 billion generated by the tooling industries. These vast economic numbers are creating a fierce international competition, with constant monitoring of and acting on innovative trends as the only way to retain the leading position. Results from related scientific areas give new impulse to well-known approaches in metal cutting or machining and to identify new opportunities for their further improvement.

Machining efficiency is a crucial area, where industry can gain a cutting edge. Though machining efficiency can be improved by accelerating the process conditions, this inevitably leads to adverse side-effects: increased process temperature, related faster tool deterioration, more frequent stops, and increased cost. This makes knowledge on the (control of) temperature, its distribution and other associated physical phenomena one of the key elements for successfully improving machining efficiency. While the temperature can be measured with a certain degree of success, practically all other phenomena like heat flux and its variation, heat losses to environment, etc. cannot be measured directly. Due to such reliance on the temperature many methods for measurement and modeling have been developed, with varying degree of efficiency, resolution, expense, precision, etc.

In this project, I will further develop my fully operational solver of heat balance in a cutting tool, based on the original inverse problem approach, which creates a framework for quantification of various thermal phenomena in metal cutting. In specific, I will develop it to the industrial needs of global tooling manufacturer SECO Tools AB and convert it into an innovation instrument for SECO to keep their cutting edge in the market.

Project Summary

Industrial oriented framework. Additional satellite modules

  1. TR. Thermal resistance measuring in tool assemblies.
  2. SM1. Temperature distribution within tool assembly.
  3. SM2 . Quantitative measure of effects of different tool coatings and tool materials.
  4. SM3. Smart sensor for momentary temperatures monitoring.
  5. SM4 . Quantification of effects of different cooling technologies.

Additional satellite modules. Thermal resistance (TR)

TR. For adequate FE modelling thermal resistances in tool assemblies are needed as well as accurate thermal dependent properties of materials.

Implementation. For different contacting pairs, experimental data on thermal resistances will be obtained with the help of LFA 467 HT at Lund University during planned secondment.

Delivery. Thermal resistances for different contacting pairs in tool assemblies.

Additional satellite modules. Temperature distribution (SM1)

SM1. The module aims to create a tool holder equipped by thermocouples. The thermocouples readings are using for calculation of heat flux flowing into tool assembly.

Implementation. The toolholder will be manufactured at SECO on the basis of a commercial toolholder. Thermocouples must be installed in toolholder only. Inserts remain changeable.

Delivery. The toolholder equipped by thermocouples and software for solution of heat inverse conduction problem.

Additional satellite modules. Coating investigation (SM2)

SM2. Coating and tool material module SM2 is built on the knowledge that different materials can modify the heat input by changing friction in contact; by acting as thermal barriers; influencing adhesion and secondary deformation, etc.

Implementation. For selected coated inserts, tests will be carried out at SECO Tools.

Delivery. The heat fluxes into tool body in dependence on insert coatings and cutting conditions.

Additional satellite modules. Smart sensor (SM3)

SM3. Smart Sensor satellite module SM3 is included into the project to enable registration of such momentary temperatures. The technology involves UV-lithographic printing of thermocouples as exemplified in the figure below.

Implementation. The sensor will be manufactured at Lund University by the middle of 2019 and then appropriate tests will be carried out at SECO Tools.

Delivery. The inserts equipped by printed thermocouples for registration of momentary temperatures.

Additional satellite modules. Cooling investigation (SM4)

SM4. Cooling efficiency satellite module SM4 utilizes the framework, where the heat exchange coefficient with environment (cooling media) is in-built as a variable into an iterative solver of the inverse problem. To resolve and quantify cooling efficiency two designed experimental setups are needed simultaneously: (a) dedicated tool and (b) cooling system, which maintains uniform (instead of directed) application of coolant.

Implementation. The experimental setup was manufactured and experiments for different coolant strategies are planned in autumn of this year at Lund University.

Delivery. The values of heat exchange coefficients with the environment for different cooling strategies.
